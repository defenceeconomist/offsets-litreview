1 Countertrade

Below is a table summarising the key offset/industrial participation regimes by country. Each cell uses short phrases rather than long sentences, and the final column cites the key evidence sources.

Country/Programme Approx. dates Aims Mechanisms & features Outcomes Key evidence source
Spain (F‑18 programme) 1982–1990 Justify purchase; create jobs; transfer high‑tech; build defence industry 100 % offset with categories; 40 % high‑tech & 10 % technology transfer; government covered extra costs; later shift to joint ventures Niche expertise developed; administrative burden and ambiguous criteria; eventual move toward industrial cooperation Offset used to justify F‑18 purchase with job and technology promises; high‑tech & transfer quotas and extra costs covered by government; administrative problems; later shift to industrial cooperation
Switzerland (F‑5/Peace Alps & F‑18) 1975–1990s Strengthen defence industry; train maintenance personnel; acquire production know‑how; reduce foreign dependence Licensed production & co‑production; offsets targeted to defence firms; cost premium ≤10 %; raised offset percentage (30 → 50 → 100 %); certification rules Developed maintenance, upgrade and spare‑parts capacity; initial inexperience slowed progress; concerns about cost & “creative accounting”; administrative complexity increased Licensed production benefits (training, technology, spares); early naïve expectations and delays; cost‑premium rule and accounting concerns; higher offset thresholds & certification requirements
United Kingdom (AWACS) Late 1980s Mitigate Nimrod cancellation; preserve jobs; secure high‑tech work 130 % offset mainly indirect; follow‑on orders credited; poor monitoring; minimal penalties Few jobs created (~1,279 sustained plus 113 created); many low‑tech or existing tasks counted; cost premium (~14 %); doubts about benefits and calls for stronger policy Press alleged low‑tech work counted, few jobs; monitoring found only ~1,392 jobs; cost premium around 14.4 %; little real impact
Israel (Industrial Cooperation) Mid‑1980s–present Build long‑term partnerships; strengthen high‑tech sector; obtain R&D, investment and market access Flexible offset guidelines (≥35 % of contract); high credits for R&D, capital investment & software; no mandatory direct/indirect split or penalties; pending law to make offsets mandatory; intense offset competition Numerous long‑term partnerships and technology investments; offset competition sometimes spirals (>70 %); upcoming law targets civil‑sector firms Guidelines emphasise 35 % offset, generous R&D and investment credits; planned law to make offsets mandatory; offset competition offers >70 % and raises concerns
Greece (OIB programme) 1990s–2000s Build defence industrial base; access technology; create jobs Weighted credit factors favour co‑production/licensed production; 10 % performance guarantee; obligations broken into categories; strong emphasis on local content Benefits accrue only if local industry can absorb technology; licensed production and co‑production save R&D cost and provide skilled employment; risk of unabsorbed credits OIB categories and weighting system with 10 % guarantee; co‑production benefits (R&D savings, employment, access to new tech); benefits contingent on industrial capacity
Belgium (F‑16 & subsequent offsets) Mid‑1970s onward Create jobs; build high‑tech industry; improve trade balance; political leverage Mandatory offsets; high credit for advanced technology and employment; heavy state intervention and rigidities Short‑term job creation and high‑tech growth; long‑term inefficiencies, market distortion, overcapacity and dependency; high cost premiums and lobbying Advantages: employment, high‑tech growth, trade balance; disadvantages: market distortion, overcapacity, dependency, lobbying
Canada (IRB programme) 1970s–present Support regional development; protect domestic industry; political objectives Industrial & Regional Benefits (IRBs); mandatory offsets tied to procurement; decisions often politicised; inconsistent criteria Inefficient resource allocation; inconsistent decisions; example: EH‑101 helicopter offset cancelled due to politics; limited industrial growth IRBs criticised for economic inefficiency and inconsistent decision‑making; EH‑101 cancellation shows political impediments
France (North European fighter competition) 1974 Secure fighter sale and industrial workshare; maintain domestic aerospace industry Competitive offset bidding: Dassault offered 70 % local manufacturing; General Dynamics promised 5,000 jobs, local currency payments and new plants Offsets were decisive in procurement decision; set precedent for high workshare demands in later competitions; created local jobs and plants Suppliers’ high‑offset bids during 1974 competition
Japan (post‑war licensed production) 1950s–1990s Build indigenous defence industry; absorb advanced technology; achieve autonomy Licensed production of US systems (e.g., F‑86, F‑104, Patriot); heavy technology transfer; channelled imported know‑how to domestic contractors; US export‑control friction Developed substantial domestic defence industry; successful indigenous systems (ASM‑1); export‑control disputes occasionally hindered co‑production Licensed production drove post‑war industrialisation; Pentagon denied tech transfer for Patriot software; imported know‑how channelled into indigenous systems like ASM‑1
Saudi Arabia (offset & joint‑venture schemes) 1980s–present Diversify economy; build technology base; strengthen defence capabilities Large offset obligations; joint ventures; technology transfer; long‑term partnerships; suppliers recoup R&D costs; limited skilled workforce Some joint ventures established; technology absorption hampered by lack of skills; offset purchases very expensive; risk of becoming branch factories; licensed manufacture rarely viable Technology transfer cost increases offset price; lack of workforce limits absorption, leading to branch‑factory risk; joint ventures used to access market
Australia (Defence offsets policy) Late 1980s–1990s Promote self‑reliance and local industry; ensure independent supply and support Mandatory offsets debated; emphasis on local content and industry involvement; acceptance of cost/delivery penalties for independence Mandatory offsets criticised for providing no advantage and creating supplier opportunism; local content improves self‑reliance but increases cost and delays Mandatory offsets provide no advantage and enable supplier opportunism; local involvement justified only if independence outweighs cost penalties
