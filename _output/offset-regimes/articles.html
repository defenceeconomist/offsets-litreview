Updated table including time period of offset regimes

Country Time period of offset regime (approx.) Aims of offset policy (key phrases) Main mechanisms & features Outcomes observed Key evidence source
Indonesia 1976–2014 (informal offsets); first formal policy introduced in 2012 and clarified in 2015 Build indigenous defence capability, ensure technology transfer, develop SMEs, reduce dependence on imports Informal offsets were ad hoc and case‑by‑case; formal regime mandates local content and technology transfer (≥85 % of contract value) and established the Defence Industry Policy Committee (KKIP) Informal regime left local firms as low‑value subcontractors; progress slow and fragmented Maharani & Matthews 2023
Malaysia Early 1990s onwards, with formal offset policy from 2005 Develop aerospace and defence industrial capacity, create jobs, diversify economy Ad hoc offset demands tied to big-ticket purchases since early 1990s; formal policy (2005) introduced guidelines and multipliers; offsets focus on technology transfer, training and co‑production but heavily skewed toward MRO Success stories (SME Aerospace, CTRM) integrated into global supply chains, but manufacturing accounts for only ~8 % of offset value and technology transfer hampered by export controls Balakrishnan & Matthews 2009
South Korea 1982–present; offset threshold introduced 1982 and raised in 1984, 1986 and 2006; offset banking and reforms introduced after 2018 Achieve self‑reliant defence industry, promote exports, build SMEs Mandatory offsets (50 % of competitive contracts valued ≥USD 1 m initially); thresholds increased over time; requirements lowered to 30 % for non‑competitive contracts; co‑production and technology transfer; offset banking scheme allows accumulation across projects Localisation of R&D and MRO capabilities; shift from licensed assembly to indigenous production; recent decline in offset value (2016–2020) spurred calls for reinforced policy Jang 2023
Turkey Early 2000s strategic plan (targeting 50 % indigenous production by 2010 and 50 % offset returns on procurement agreements ≥USD 10 m) Build industrial self‑reliance and export capability, secure technology transfer Mandatory offsets and co‑production; preference for suppliers offering export licences and flexible technology transfer Encouraged local production (e.g., T129 helicopter) but created friction with U.S. export‑control laws and pushed Turkey toward alternative suppliers Zanotti 2011
Poland / Central & Eastern Europe Offsets used since the 1990s, but renewed emphasis 2022–present following Russia’s invasion of Ukraine Balance rapid readiness with long‑term self‑sufficiency, shorten supply chains Government‑to‑government deals: initial off‑the‑shelf deliveries followed by domestic component production, licensed production and MRO; extended timelines allow suppliers to spread obligations Enabled rapid procurement while building local production and MRO capacity; offsets resumed after temporary pause post‑2022 Anicetti 2025
Switzerland Long‑standing offset regime (in place at least since the 1990s; precise start date not specified) Maintain domestic defence know‑how, reduce foreign dependence, ensure political acceptability of procurement budgets Requires reciprocal trade equal to 100 % of contract value; mix of direct and indirect offsets confined to competitive Swiss suppliers; cost premia of ~4 % may be paid for local co‑production Sustained local defence industry and supplier base; modest cost premia justified by through‑life savings Markowski 1998
Czech Republic Offset policy implemented 1998 (Decree 421/1998); Gripen Industrial Cooperation Programme ran 2004–2015 Build industrial base, integrate SMEs, gain economic benefits Industrial Cooperation Programme for Gripen: at least 100 % offset with ≥20 % direct; 10‑year programme; multi‑ministry offset commission; MoD final authority After two years, 26 % of obligations delivered; Czech firms integrated into supply chains; success linked to comprehensive agreement and coordinated oversight Amara & Pargač 2009
Hungary Gripen offset programme 2001–2015 (planned 14‑year programme completed in 7 years) Similar aims as Czech Republic (jobs, industrial development) Offsets valued at 110 % of agreement; programme regulated by government decisions; focused on investment and export commitments Resources concentrated in developed regions; defence industry excluded; firms went bankrupt; programme widely considered a failure Lazar 2019
Portugal Offsets pursued mainly in the 2000s; precise timeframe not specified Foster technology and industrial development Programme included direct, defence‑industry indirect and non‑defence indirect offsets; lack of coordination among ministries and vague contract terms Offsets underperformed; suppliers preferred paying fines over compliance Santos et al. 2023
South Africa Offsets tied to 1999 Arms Deal with obligations running through the 2000s (data covers 2000–2020) Create jobs, develop domestic industry, attract investment Defence Industrial Participation (DIP) and National Industrial Participation (NIP) programmes; scoring system manipulated via high multipliers, package deals and generous causality interpretations Credits far exceeded real investment; limited economic benefit; some projects collapsed Holden 2020
Brazil Gripen offset programme begun 2013 and ongoing Achieve self‑reliant aviation industry, secure technology transfer Offset package valued at 175 % of contract; 100 % technology transfer; 80 % of aircraft structure built in Brazil; deferred counter‑contract (Sweden buys Brazilian aircraft) Ambitious technology transfer but success depends on Brazil’s absorptive capacity; industrial benefits uncertain Herrara 2008
Singapore Offset regime dates to the 2000s (case‑by‑case negotiation; no prescriptive timeframe) Build advanced defence ecosystem, ensure supply autonomy Negotiates offsets privately on a case‑by‑case basis; strong central agency (DSTA); collaborations with foreign partners on R&D; local construction of warships and joint research Successful local production (Formidable‑class frigates) and acquisition of complex technologies; defence industry highly integrated into global supply chains Matthews & De Vera 2019
United Kingdom Industrial Participation policy developed in the 1980s and still in force Maintain industrial base while avoiding excessive offset burdens Suppliers propose industrial benefits equal to 100 % of offshore content; no multipliers; competitive evaluation; technology offsets accepted but price loading discouraged Policy considered efficient; exporters face “Hobson’s choice” (offer offsets or lose business) Matthews 1991
European Union (policy) Common defence procurement rules since 2009 with Directive 2009/81/EC restricting offsets Reduce market distortions and increase cooperation within European Defence Technological and Industrial Base (EDTIB) Directive restricts offsets to essential security interests; Commission challenges national offset schemes through case law; supply‑chain compensation offered to smaller states National offset rules revised; offsets declining; cooperation and competitive procurement fostered Weiss & Blauberger 2016
